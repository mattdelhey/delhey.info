On loneliness

Recently questioned on how to avoid loneliness when digging yourself into a hole, i.e. the way I live my life. What follows is my saccharine response. You don’t. It’s no secret that the whole monogamous partnership thing is primarily motivated out of a fear of being alone, despite our pretentious to the contrary regarding the beauty of love, etc. (although these shouldn’t be written of entirely, they are just not the fundamental ground for the behavior). For me, I’ve felt alone for so long that I’ve developed what I imagine are typical coping mechanisms: a thicker skin, a richer appreciation for and greater reliance on the true intimacies that I do have, a rejection of the bourgeoise demands to ‘stay safe and have fun’ and a recognition of the failure of society to live up to its grandiose promises (the short term solution to which is an open acceptance of the poverty of daily life).

The dramas of music and cinema before the completion of today’s global capital give examples of what I mean. For example Greil Marcus uses the phrase “the old, weird america” to describe “the often eerie country, blues, and folk music feature on the Anthology of American Folk Music”. This type of drama emphasizes that death is waiting around the corner and turns towards more essential shapes of life (religion, family, catastrophe) in order to make sense and value in the world. Loneliness is given a full treatment and is openly discussed. I think you can find this in a lot of places, especially in something like the Torah. This is a stark contrast to the claims of the so-called interconnectedness that we are supposed to attribute to ourselves today. The economic reality around us one that secretly feeds on the isolation of individuals while proclaiming to bring absolute connectedness to the world (which is, of course, in some senses absolutely true).

Loneliness is more or less essential to life (at least as we conceive it today) and to try and avoid it entirely is to miss the point, similar to pain. One can trade in some kinds of loneliness (e.g. always surround themselves with others) but this comes at well-known costs. So, at least for now, one must be lonely, But for me, this open admission of loneliness brings with it a liberation from the truly impressioning pretensions to the contrary (e.g. going to see avengers with friends your don’t even really like in order to not be alone) and encourages a fuller appreciation for the historic struggles of those similar to us, shining light on our own subjugation to an unknown system (and thus also the paths of escape). This doesn’t mean throw out your friends and your lovers; it means ruthlessly filter and then fully appreciate. Unfortunately I’m still stuck on the former.

Another and final perspective: clearly I’ve come to see myself as a complete outsider, perhaps even a sort of terrorist, to popular society. I openly mock, experience extreme nausea from, and overall despise many behaviors often considered entirely ‘normal’ (e.g. facebook). By understanding myself as entirely Other, I “rationalize” my loneliness, I find it easier and more acceptable that I’m often alone. The point of bringing this perspective up is not to praise it but to point out its problems. Such a perspective is ultimately at the whims of that which I despise. The conclusion to be drawn at the end is the same at the beginning: as far as I know, there are no solutions to loneliness as we currently experience it. But that doesn’t stop me from going to underground techno parties and dreaming of art history majors, or from finding real comfort in my attempts to historically and theoretically understand loneliness as I suffer from its causes and symptoms.

Behind us is a film on modernity versus tradition. Tradition is the assemblage of the shapes of life and concepts that determine the legitimate ways of judging for a group, the normatively of a community. Tradition is the arbitrary that develops itself beyond the arbitrary, erasing the incommensurable to pave way for the judicable, especially in cases in which praxis demands an answer. As we know, modernity is the edifice of the incommensurable, which ultimately replaces the task of “seeing things as they really are” with “how things seem to us”, or more accurately, the unity of the two perspectives, of the objective and the subjective, of the infinite and the finite, of being and essence. The work of modernity is the field in which the pinnacle of judgement arrises, finding only its lower developments on each side of history.
